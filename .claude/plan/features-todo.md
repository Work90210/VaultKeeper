Cryptographic cross-border evidence exchange with selective disclosure. Today when an NGO shares evidence with a prosecutor, or a prosecutor shares with defence, they export a bundle and pray. A protocol where VaultKeeper instance A can cryptographically hand an evidence subset to VaultKeeper instance B with proof that the handed-over subset is authentic, complete with respect to a declared scope, and hasn't been modified — that's a federation primitive no one else will build because no one else is sovereign-by-construction. Bellingcat → Lighthouse → ICC list counsel → national prosecutor can all share without any of them trusting a central cloud. This is the feature that makes VaultKeeper network-effect-y rather than just a single-tenant tool.
Witness protection threat model as a first-class product area. You've got AES-256-GCM witness ID encryption and safety profiles. That's table stakes. The unique version is: duress access control (second passphrase produces a decoy vault), deniable witness records for investigators operating in hostile jurisdictions, automated threat-modeled redaction suggestions (detects when a combination of metadata could re-identify a source), and cryptographic revocation of a witness's testimony from an entire case tree with proof-of-removal for all downstream derivatives. This is the feature set that matters when an investigator is physically at risk and their laptop gets seized at a border. Bellingcat, Mnemonic, Airwars all have real operational exposure here and no tool addresses it.
Formal verification of the chain-of-custody engine. Nobody in the DEMS market has done this. Taking the core chain-of-custody logic and formally verifying it with TLA+ or Coq, publishing the proofs, and shipping the verified binary — that's a defensibility layer that costs Relativity's legal team nothing to claim but costs them years to actually achieve. For ICC-level admissibility challenges, "formally verified" is a phrase that survives cross-examination in ways "industry-standard" doesn't. This is a lot of work but it's a once-and-done moat.
Sealed evidence escrow with time-locked or multi-party release. A victim testifies to Bellingcat today but the evidence shouldn't be released until either (a) the regime falls, (b) a multi-party quorum unlocks it, or (c) a dead-man's-switch timer expires. Threshold cryptography makes this trivially implementable. No current DEMS supports it. This is specifically the kind of feature that unlocks truth commission, transitional justice, and exile-based documentation work.
Air-gapped deployment with cryptographic sync to connected instances. The highest-security tier of your customers (IIIM Syria, UNITAD-adjacent bodies, exile-based documentation groups working in diaspora) cannot run internet-connected infrastructure. Offering a fully air-gapped deployment mode that can later cryptographically reconcile with a connected VaultKeeper instance — with provable non-tampering during the air-gap period — is a capability that costs Relativity nothing to refuse because their revenue model depends on cloud. It costs you relatively little because you're self-hostable by design.
Jurisdiction-aware processing pipeline. Some evidence types cannot legally leave certain jurisdictions, even within the EU. German BDSG requirements differ from Dutch AVG implementation. Italian investigative secrecy rules differ from Belgian rules. A VaultKeeper instance that can enforce "this evidence item can be processed by services in these jurisdictions only" at the infrastructure level — and cryptographically prove compliance to an auditor — is a feature every pan-European investigation needs and nobody has.
